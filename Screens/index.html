A world full of screens

Cutting through the media clutter

The screen is the modern day canvas, ready to be painted with vibrant pixels. Only an artists imagination restricts its contents. However, in this day and age we're so used to these flickering paintings that we barely notice them. We may glance at a screen at the mall while waiting in line, but chances are low that we remember it afterwards. Even if it's a commercial with high production value and millions in budget. Maybe even especially if it happens to be that way. And no wonder! Our lives are filled with screens and there is no lack of, let's say, car commercials with impressive helicopter shots. It's still just another ad to us.
Of course, this problem is no news to any of us. Cutting through the media clutter has been a challenge for content creators ever since cavemen begun painting on walls (presumably).
So what is our approach?

While the content of a screen is obviously important, the context of where it sits is most often overlooked. Our background in live productions has shaped our thought-process differently. Where we're from, screens are used as building blocks of larger environments. They are props in a scenery, used to enhance the story we are about to tell. When we're about to fill a screen, the first question we ask ourselves is actually not what to put in it. Rather: Where is it? Who's there? When? What is the atmosphere like?

Immersive experiences

A screen doesn't have to be just a flat rectangle.

We often think of a screen as simply a flat rectangular surface, it doesn't have to be that way.

For many years, we've been experimenting with screens as building blocks of larger environments. By putting some thought into the the shape of a screen and the context of where it sits, you're pretty much guaranteed to get eye-popping results.
